Why considering reuse?


Enabling reuse
  • facilitates data sharing and collaborations
  • increases the visibility of research and can lead to more citations
  • improves the transparency, reliability and replication of research
  • [...]

and thereby
  • maximises potential from data assets
  • maximises research impact
https://www.howtofair.dk/why-fair/
Conditions that apply to digital scholarly editions
where possible, it should attend to possibilities
of sampling, reuse, and remix, supporting approaches
to the formation and curation of the edition such as
reconstructing and documenting instances of texts
and textual change over time, like algorithmic
construction and reconstruction (with possible
extensibility, including external data)
Modern Language Association of America. 2016. ‘MLA Statement on the Scholarly Edition in the Digital Age’.

What to reuse



  • Code / tools
  • Data

For what to reuse



  • extract information from the data for reusing in a non computational context. E.g.: writing an article, preparing a print dictionary. Human consumption

  • reuse the data in a digital context. E.g.: further processing or annotating, compiling with other dataset. Machine consumption

What to reuse and for what (examples)



  • Text (documentary, diplomatic, critical, etc.) → text analysis (literary, linguistic, historical), scholarly editing (collation, larger corpus), training data
  • Archival documents (manuscripts, publications, etc.) → catalogues
  • Entities → prosopography, gazetteers
  • All → replication (peer review, update)
  • ...

    From the APIs analysis ...


    • Variety of formats: xml-tei, but also different flavours of json; limited support for txt (cf. Franzini et al. 2019, 6.16)

    • Retrieve by content or by structure: ideally DTS combined with project API for metadata, fulltext search, specific content

    • Authority file records: enrichment and linking of the dataset

    • Curated or raw data: consider human and machine consumption

    The open question of versioning


    Much of the OBAPI documentation is also useful for understanding the XML files, as the API is based on the same data. However, please note that sometimes the files on ORDA and the API might represent slightly different versions of the data.
    The Proceedings of the Old Bailey, https://www.oldbaileyonline.org/static/Data.jsp

    NB: That dump file is a snapshot of the database, from a point in time, and is not most recent version of the live database; to access the most recent data, please refer to the REST API.
    Registres de la Comédie-Française, https://hack.cfregisters.org/en/receipts/database.html

    Conclusions (1)


    Versioning and replication: API, content of data dump and website may change over time, which limits replication


    The multi-tasking textual scholar: should scholars become familiar also with APIs consumption (and development)?

    Conclusions (2)

    Toward standardization in editions APIs?

    For xml-tei texts: DTS!

    For content queries: does it make sense to define a common set of entities (e.g. FRBR and named entities)?
